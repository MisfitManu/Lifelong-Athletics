Your Article Title Goes Here

A one or two sentence summary that draws the reader in. Make it specific and intriguing.

6 min read

This is your opening paragraph. Start strong — hook the reader immediately with a surprising fact, a common misconception, or a direct statement of what they're going to learn. Don't ease in slowly. Get to the point.

This is your second paragraph. Expand on the opening. Give context. Why does this topic matter? Who is it relevant to? Keep sentences short and punchy.

"This is a callout box — use it for a key insight, a striking stat, or a memorable one-liner that you want to stand out."

Your First Section Heading

This is the body text under your first section. Write in short paragraphs — 3 to 5 sentences max. Your reader is on a phone or taking a break. Make it easy to consume.

Add another paragraph here. Go deeper into the point. Use plain language — you're not writing a textbook. You're explaining science to someone who wants to understand it, not memorise it.

  • First point — keep each item to one clear sentence
  • Second point — start with a verb when possible
  • Third point — make each item stand on its own
  • Fourth point — 3 to 5 items is ideal, more than 6 loses attention

Your Second Section Heading

Keep writing. Another 2-3 paragraphs here. Remember — each section should answer one question or make one point clearly.

A Subheading If You Need One

Use h3 when a section has multiple distinct parts that need their own label. Don't overuse — only when the reader genuinely needs a signpost.

Key Takeaway

Summarise the most important point of this section in 1-2 sentences. What should the reader remember and apply?

Your Third Section Heading

Continue writing. Aim for 800–1200 words total for a solid blog post. Under 600 feels thin. Over 1500 needs to be truly exceptional to hold attention.

End this section with a clear transition toward your conclusion. What should the reader do with this information?

  1. First step or first reason — be specific
  2. Second step or second reason
  3. Third step or third reason

Your closing paragraph. Bring it back to the reader — what's the one thing they should take away and act on? End with something that resonates, not just "hope this helped."
